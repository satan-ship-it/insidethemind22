The Construct of Self

The "Self" is the totality of your conscious experience. This section breaks down how we perceive ourselves, from biological roots to cultural influences.

Subject vs. Object

The self is unique because it is both the observer and the observed.

Self as Subject (The Knower)

The entity that acts.
Example: "I am a dancer."

Self as Object (The Known)

The entity that is observed or affected.
Example: "I am easily hurt."

Social Self

Emerges through interaction. Values cooperation, sacrifice, unity, and sharing. Often called familial or relational self.

Key Constructs

  • Self-Concept

    How we perceive ourselves and our competencies.

  • Self-Efficacy (Bandura)

    Belief in one's ability to control life outcomes.

  • Self-Regulation

    Organizing and monitoring one's own behavior.

Focus: Self-Esteem

A value judgment of one's own worth. By age 6-7, it forms in four distinct areas:

Academic Competence
Social Competence
Physical Competence
Physical Appearance
Impact: High esteem correlates with performance. Low esteem links to anxiety.

Personality Frameworks

Personality refers to stable qualities ("mask") characterizing behavior. Use the filter to explore historical approaches.

The Big Five (Five-Factor Model)

Considered the most promising empirical approach, this model suggests personality exists along a continuum of five broad dimensions (O.C.E.A.N.). Unlike types, which are distinct categories, traits are degrees of attributes.
